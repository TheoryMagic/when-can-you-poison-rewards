When Can You Poison Rewards?

A Tight Characterization of Reward Poisoning in Linear MDPs

Jose E. Aguilar Escamilla, Haoyang Hong, Jiawei Li, Haoyu Zhao, Xuezhou Zhang, Sanghyun Hong, Huazheng Wang

We characterize when a target policy can be induced by reward poisoning with a bounded attack budget—and when the instance is intrinsically robust.

Reward model basics

RL learns from reward feedback.

A reward function assigns scalar feedback to state–action choices. In modern RL, those reward signals can come from human feedback or interaction data, which may be noisy, imperfect, or strategically manipulated.

Where reward poisoning enters

The learner observes feedback and updates its policy. A reward-poisoning adversary modifies the observed reward signal, so the learner updates from r̂ instead of the clean reward r.

Linear-MDP reward model. In the paper, rewards have the form rh(s,a)=φ(s,a)⊤θh. Poisoning can be read as changing the reward parameters toward θh†.

Main result

Attackability reduces to one CQP.

For a fixed target policy π†, the CQP asks whether reward perturbation can shift the optimal Q-function in the attacker’s favor within the linear feature span.

Scope. For every stage h, every state s with dπ†h(s) > 0, and every rival action a ≠ π†(s).

The sign is the certificate

ε* > 0
Attackable / vulnerable

A budget-efficient reward-poisoning strategy exists against a broad class of no-regret learners.

ε* ≤ 0
Intrinsically robust

Any successful attack requires a budget that grows linearly with the horizon, even against standard non-robust algorithms.

How to read the certificate
Positive gapThe CQP finds a reward shaping that opens a strict Q-value gap for π†.
Non-positive gapThe target cannot be separated from its competitors within the allowed shaping class.

Intrinsic robustness

Not just algorithmic robustness.

The paper’s distinction is that robustness can also come from the underlying environment geometry, not only from robust estimators, detectors, or defenses.

Algorithmic robustness

Design learners that tolerate corruption. Safety is a property of the algorithm.

Intrinsic robustness

Some attack objectives are impossible to achieve without large costs. Safety is a property of the environment.

Attack procedures

Two attacks, same CQP idea.

This part is intentionally brief on the website; the paper gives the algorithms and guarantees.

White-box

Knows the linear-MDP model. Perturb only when the learner deviates from π†; target-action rewards are left untouched. Total budget is sublinear on attackable instances.

Black-box

Estimates the model first, then applies a CQP-based test and attack. The slide summary uses a two-stage procedure: nudge toward π†, then penalize rivals.

Empirical evidence

CQP prediction matches poisoning behavior.

Deep RL environments are approximated as linear MDPs through learned linear representations; then the CQP prediction is compared with actual poisoning outcomes.

Vulnerable / attackable

Policy difference converges toward zero; cumulative perturbation tapers as the learner follows the target more often.

Robust

Policy difference lacks convergence; perturbations do not decrease over time, indicating the adversary fails to induce the target behavior.

Takeaways

What the page should leave the reader with.

Intrinsic robustness in linear MDPs

Some target policies cannot be induced without large poisoning costs.

One convex test

The sign of ε* decides attackability under necessary and sufficient conditions.

Practical relevance

The characterization predicts outcomes in learned linear representations of deep-RL benchmarks.
